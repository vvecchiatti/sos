Ötillö World Series Cannes 2022

2022 · Söuthsea Swimrun

Having had a pretty difficult 12 months, I finally found myself with the headspace to think about racing another world series swimrun. The Ötillö Cannes course had been on my radar for a while. Warm seas, sunny days, the South of France in October. Count me in.

Inspired by ultrarunner Damian Hall's book In It For the Long Run, I decided to travel by train rather than fly. London, Eurostar to Paris, overnight train toward Nice, arriving in Cannes 24 hours later. Approximately 46kg CO2e instead of 350kg — and about £100 each way.

The world series course — 32km running, 8km swimming, 22 sections — starts on Île Sainte-Marguerite, a 15-minute ferry from Cannes, within the old Fort Royal that once imprisoned the Man in the Iron Mask.

The race began when the fort's gates were opened. We ran a lap of the island's pine and eucalyptus trails, swam around the fort, then crossed to the neighbouring Île Saint-Honorat. The swim back passed directly over underwater sculptures of Cannes residents' heads, created by artist Jason deCaires Taylor — a new home for local sea life.

After an almost 5km run back across Île Sainte-Marguerite, we reached the 1300m swim back to the mainland — the longest of the race. I set off high on swimrun life and promptly caught a purple jellyfish inside my right elbow on the catch phase of my stroke. I let out a significant yelping noise. I then spent the next kilometre getting progressively more paranoid that a bit of jellyfish was still attached to my arm. Basically, it really hurt. But I might also be a bit of a drama queen.

A kind lifeguard rubbed some cream on my sting. Bending my arm hurt. Thank god I only had 30km left to race.

What followed was a spectacular run through Cannes itself — along the Croisette, through the shopping streets, over a forest trail, past a nudist beach (occupied, mercifully), and back to the finish on the seafront.

The Cannes World Series is unlike any other swimrun course. If you get the chance, take the train and go race it.

By Sarah King.
